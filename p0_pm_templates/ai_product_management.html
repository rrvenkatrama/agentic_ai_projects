PART 1 — The PM Mindset

What a Product Manager Actually Does

The role is often misunderstood. Here's what a PM does and doesn't do:

DoesDoesn't (in modern orgs)
Decides WHAT to build and WHYWrite code (some do, but it's not the job)
Owns the success metrics for a product/featureManage engineers (that's the EM/manager)
Talks to customers continuously (discovery)Run sprints (that's the EM/Scrum Master)
Prioritizes ruthlessly (says NO often)Design the UI (that's the designer)
Communicates with stakeholders (eng, sales, exec)Write tests / QA (that's QA + engineers)
Defines success metrics, eval criteria, kill thresholds"Manage the project" (TPMs/PgMs do that — see below)
Owns the strategic narrative (positioning, GTM)Sell the product (that's sales)

A PM's superpower is making prioritization decisions with incomplete information and getting alignment around them. The artifacts (PRDs, ICPs) are tools for that decision-making — not the job itself.

PM vs TPM vs PgM

Three roles often confused, especially relevant given your TPM background:

RoleOwnsAsks
PM (Product Manager) What and why — outcomes "What should we build for whom and why?"
TPM (Technical Program Manager) How and when — execution "How do we ship this on time, across teams?"
PgM (Program Manager) Cross-team coordination "How do we orchestrate multiple programs?"
Your background advantage
Coming from a 12-year Apple TPM background, you already have execution chops, stakeholder skills, and roadmap discipline. The shift to AI PM is mainly: (1) less execution focus, (2) more outcome focus, (3) heavy AI-specific concepts (evals, model selection, hallucination management). Your TPM muscle memory accelerates the move.

AI PM vs Traditional PM

AspectTraditional PMAI PM
PredictabilitySpec → outcome is largely predictableSpec → outcome requires experimentation
Cycle timeMonths per releaseDays/weeks per prompt iteration
Eval criteriaFunctional tests, A/B on conversionQuality metrics (precision, faithfulness, hallucination rate)
Cost variabilityStable (engineering hours)Highly variable (per-token costs)
Failure modesBugs, performance, edge casesHallucinations, drift, prompt injection, model deprecation
Spec confidenceHigh before buildLow until spike measures behavior
Tools used dailyMixpanel, Jira, FigmaLangSmith, Pinecone dashboards, prompt eval tools

Three Workflow Models

Model 1 — Waterfall (Traditional / Large Enterprise)

Strategy → Market Research → ICP → PRD → Spec → Engineering → QA → Launch → Measure
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────→
   (months of PM/research)        (months of engineering)         (post-launch)

Common at: Apple infrastructure, Oracle, large enterprise. Slow but predictable. Not how AI products typically work.

Model 2 — Lean Startup

Hypothesis → Quick PRD → Thin-slice MVP → Measure → Pivot or Expand
       ↺────────────────────────────────────────────↺
                  (continuous loop, weekly/monthly)

Common at: most modern SaaS startups. PM and engineering interleave tightly. PRDs are 1 page, not 30.

Model 3 — Modern AI Product Workflow

Hypothesis → Spike (engineering tries it) → Measure model behavior →
   Real PRD (now with data) → Build → Closed alpha → Iterate → GA
                                          ↑
                          You can't write a credible PRD until
                          the spike shows the model can actually do it

Dominant at: OpenAI, Anthropic, modern AI startups. Engineering "spike" comes BEFORE the real PRD because AI behavior isn't predictable from spec.

Why Path B mimics Model 3
In your training, P7-P12 engineering work IS the spike. The PM artifact tickets (#81-#86) are written AFTER the engineering, which gives you real numbers (precision, cost, latency, failure modes) to write credible PRDs. That's exactly how AI PMs work in practice.
PART 2 — Core Terminology Glossary
PRD — Product Requirements Document
A document that captures what you're building, for whom, and how you'll measure success. Modern lean PRDs are 1 PAGE, not 30.
Lean structure: Problem · Hypothesis · ICP · Success metrics · MVP scope · Kill criteria · Open questions. You'll write one for every project P7-P12.
ICP — Ideal Customer Profile
The SPECIFIC type of customer that gets the most value from your product. Not "who could use it" — "who SHOULD we sell to first." Always paired with an Anti-ICP (who we are EXPLICITLY NOT for).
Good ICP: "B2B SaaS, Series B+, 100-2000 customers, CSM team of 5-30, recently lost a $1M+ account." Bad ICP: "Anyone who uses LLMs."
Spike
A time-boxed engineering experiment to learn something or de-risk a hypothesis. Throwaway code is fine. Goal is information, not shipping.
"Spike: can Claude reliably extract dates from contracts? 1 week. Success criteria: 90% precision on 100-doc test set." If yes, write the real PRD. If no, pivot the approach.
MVP — Minimum Viable Product
The SMALLEST thing you can ship to test your core hypothesis. NOT "version 1" — it's deliberately incomplete. MLP (Minimum Lovable Product) is the modern version: minimum that customers actually love, not just tolerate.
MVP for ChurnGuard: detects 1 churn signal (login recency), alerts via email. NOT: 12 signals + dashboard + Salesforce integration + mobile app.
JTBD — Jobs To Be Done
A framework that asks: "What is the customer 'hiring' this product to DO for them?" Focuses on the underlying job, not features.
Customers don't hire a CRM — they hire a "stop my deals from slipping through cracks" job. Reframing problems as jobs reveals competitive alternatives (a smart CSM might do the job too — that's your real competitor).
GTM — Go-To-Market
The strategy + execution plan for how a product reaches customers. Includes positioning, pricing, channels, sales motion, launch sequencing.
GTM brief for ChurnGuard: "Direct sales to VP CS at 200 mid-market SaaS targets. Pricing $50K-$150K ACV. Channel: Pavilion + Gainsight community + LinkedIn outreach. Pilot motion: 90-day paid pilot."
North Star Metric
The ONE metric that best captures product success. Everything else is secondary. Should reflect customer value, not vanity.
Slack: "Daily active users sending 2000+ messages per workspace." Not "signups." Reflects actual engagement. For AI products, often a quality+adoption hybrid.
OKRs — Objectives & Key Results
Goal-setting framework. Objective = qualitative ambition. Key Results = 3-5 measurable outcomes that prove you achieved the objective.
Objective: "Make ChurnGuard the default churn-prediction tool for mid-market SaaS." KR1: 50 paying customers by Q4. KR2: NPS 40+. KR3: 70% precision on churn predictions.
KPIs — Key Performance Indicators
Ongoing metrics tracked to monitor product health. Different from OKRs (which are time-bound goals). KPIs are the dashboard you check daily.
KPIs for an AI agent: daily active users, tokens consumed per user, precision/recall on golden test set, p95 latency, cost per call, NPS, churn rate.
TAM / SAM / SOM
TAM (Total Addressable Market) = entire market for the category. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) = the slice you can realistically serve. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) = what you can capture in 3-5 years.
TAM: all churn prevention software ($5B). SAM: B2B SaaS mid-market ($800M). SOM: realistic 3-year capture ($30M).
ACV / ARR / MRR
ACV (Annual Contract Value) = avg contract size per customer per year. ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) = total recurring revenue annualized. MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue) = same, monthly. SaaS metrics — used to size deals and growth.
ChurnGuard: $100K ACV × 50 customers = $5M ARR. MRR = $417K.
CAC / LTV
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = cost to win one new customer. LTV (Lifetime Value) = total revenue from a customer over their lifetime. Healthy SaaS: LTV/CAC > 3.
CAC = $20K (sales + marketing). LTV = $300K (3-year retention × $100K ACV). Ratio = 15. Very healthy.
RICE / MoSCoW Prioritization
Frameworks for prioritizing roadmap items. RICE = Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. MoSCoW = Must-have / Should-have / Could-have / Won't-have.
Use RICE when you have data to estimate. Use MoSCoW for quick stakeholder alignment without quantification.
PMF — Product-Market Fit
The state where customers actively want and use your product. Hard to measure precisely; usually you "know it when you see it" (Sean Ellis test: "would you be very disappointed if you couldn't use this?" — 40%+ saying yes = PMF).
Pre-PMF: "I built it, hard to get customers to try." Post-PMF: "Customers refer their friends; sales are easier; usage grows organically."
User Persona vs Buyer Persona
User = the person who USES the product daily. Buyer = the person who DECIDES to purchase. Often different in B2B.
ChurnGuard: User = Customer Success Manager (uses the dashboard daily). Buyer = VP Customer Success (signs the contract). Marketing/sales materials must speak to BOTH.
Customer Discovery
The process of validating problems and solutions with REAL customers (not assumptions). Continuous, not one-shot. Often the most-skipped, highest-leverage activity.
Talk to 5-10 ICP customers per week. Ask about THEIR problems, not your solution. "Tell me about the last time you lost an account unexpectedly..." — open-ended, learns about real pain.
Roadmap (Now / Next / Later)
Modern roadmaps avoid Gantt charts and dates. Three buckets: Now (in flight), Next (next 1-2 quarters), Later (rest). Communicates direction without false precision.
Now: ChurnGuard core MVP. Next: Salesforce integration, mobile alerts. Later: Predictive churn-cause analysis, automated intervention recommendations.
Engineering Ping-Pong
Tight back-and-forth between PM and engineering during build. PM defines a slice → engineering builds → measure → PM refines based on what was learned → engineering iterates. Replaces big upfront specs.
"Engineering shipped a churn detector. Precision is 45%. PM: that's not enough. What if we add 3 more signals? Eng: estimates 1 week. PM: do it. Measure again. Now 62%. Closer."
Design Partner / Closed Alpha / Beta
Design Partner = early customer who works closely with you (often free or discounted) in exchange for input. Closed Alpha = 5-20 selected customers, very early. Beta = wider rollout, near production.
Stage progression: 5 design partners → 50-100 beta → public GA. Each stage has different goals: alpha = find PMF; beta = scale ops; GA = grow.
Working Backwards (Amazon)
Famous Amazon process: write the LAUNCH PRESS RELEASE before building anything. If the press release isn't compelling enough to drive customer action, the idea isn't ready.
Press release for ChurnGuard's launch — written in customer-language, with hypothetical customer quotes. Forces clarity about the OUTCOME (not features).
RAIL / AARRR (Pirate Metrics)
AARRR = Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral → Revenue. The classic SaaS funnel. RAIL = Recommended Action, Insight, Learning — a more recent variant.
Acquisition: how do customers find us? Activation: do they get value in first session? Retention: do they come back next week? Referral: do they tell others? Revenue: do they pay/expand?
PART 3 — AI-Specific PM Concepts

Eval-Driven Development

The AI equivalent of TDD. Before you build the AI feature, you build the evaluation harness — a way to measure whether the model behavior is good enough.

# Workflow:
1. Define success metrics (precision >= 70%, false positives < 5/week)
2. Build eval dataset (golden inputs + expected outputs)
3. Build the AI feature
4. Run evals
5. Iterate prompts/models/architecture until evals pass

Without an eval harness, you're shipping vibes. Tools: Ragas, DeepEval, Promptfoo, LangSmith.

Prompt Iteration as Product Releases

In AI products, tuning a prompt IS shipping a release. A prompt change affects every customer interaction. Track prompt versions like code versions:

Prompt versionDateEval scoreNotes
v1.02026-05-0162% precisionInitial
v1.12026-05-0868%Added "step-by-step" instruction
v1.22026-05-1571%Few-shot examples added

PMs own the prompt iteration roadmap, not just code features.

Model Selection as a PM Decision

Choosing Claude Opus 4.7 vs GPT-4o vs Gemini Ultra is a PM tradeoff (not just engineering):

DimensionWhat PM considers
QualityEval scores on YOUR test set (not benchmarks)
CostPer-token cost × expected usage
Latencyp95 response time — affects UX
ReliabilityUptime SLA, rate limits
Vendor riskLock-in, deprecation history
ComplianceHIPAA-eligible? Data residency?

Often the best answer is route by request type: cheap model for simple cases, premium model for complex ones. PM defines the routing rules.

Cost-per-Task as a North Star

Token costs add up fast. PMs track cost-per-task as a first-class metric alongside conversion/retention.

cost_per_task = (input_tokens × $/1M_in) + (output_tokens × $/1M_out)

# ChurnGuard example:
# - 5 churn signals analyzed per customer
# - Each signal: ~2000 input tokens, ~500 output tokens
# - Claude Opus 4.7: $15/$75 per 1M tokens
# - cost = (5 × 2000 × $15/1M) + (5 × 500 × $75/1M)
# - cost = $0.15 + $0.19 = $0.34 per customer per analysis
# - 1000 customers × daily analysis = $340/day = $124K/year

If you're charging $50/customer/month and burning $10 in tokens, your gross margin is destroyed. PMs design pricing around this.

Hallucination Budget

Acknowledging that LLMs WILL sometimes be wrong — and pre-deciding how much wrong is acceptable.

Use caseAcceptable hallucination rate
Marketing copy generation5-10% (human reviews anyway)
Code suggestions10-20% (developer accepts/rejects)
Customer support drafts2-5% (with human approval gate)
Legal contract analysis< 0.5% (compounds liability)
Medical advice~0% (effectively can't ship without human in loop)

The PM defines the budget. Engineering builds to it (RAG for grounding, confidence thresholds, human-in-loop for high-stakes).

Confidence Thresholds & Kill Switches

Two safety mechanisms PMs design:

  • Confidence threshold: only auto-execute if model confidence > X. Below that, escalate to human or refuse.
  • Kill switch: ability to instantly disable the AI feature when something goes wrong (eval drift, cost runaway, customer complaints).
# Pseudo-pattern:
prediction, confidence = agent.predict(input)
if confidence > 0.85:
    auto_act(prediction)
elif confidence > 0.5:
    queue_for_human_review(prediction)
else:
    decline_to_predict()
    log_for_analysis()

Data Flywheel

The compound advantage of usage-data-feeding-quality-improvements:

Users → Use product → Generate data → Train better models / improve prompts →
                                                    ↓
                                          Better product → More users → ...

PMs design feedback loops to capture data ethically (with consent) and use it to improve quality. Differentiator vs new entrants.

Grounding & Retrieval (RAG)

Grounding = forcing the model to answer based on specific provided context (not its general training). Reduces hallucination.

The technique: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Given a question, retrieve relevant documents, include them in the prompt, ask the model to answer based ONLY on those docs.

PM responsibility: define what "groundedness" means for your product. Tools: Ragas measures groundedness automatically.

LLM-as-Judge for Eval

Use one LLM to evaluate another LLM's output. Cheap, scalable, replaces some human grading.

# Example LLM-as-judge prompt:
"Given the user query: {query}
And the assistant response: {response}
Rate the response on a scale of 1-5 for:
- Accuracy
- Helpfulness
- Tone
Respond in JSON: {accuracy: int, helpfulness: int, tone: int}"

PM defines the rubric (what to measure, scale). DeepEval and similar tools wrap this pattern.

A/B Testing Prompts

Same as A/B testing UX — but on prompts. Show variant A to half of users, variant B to half, measure outcome metric.

ToolUse
PromptfooSide-by-side prompt comparison on a fixed test set (offline eval)
LangSmith ExperimentsProduction A/B testing with traffic splitting
Custom feature flagsRoll your own (LaunchDarkly, etc.)

PART 5 — Working with Customers & Translating Needs into PRDs

This is the actual day-to-day work of a PM. Customers and users are the source of truth — the PM's job is to talk to them constantly, find patterns, and translate raw business needs into products that engineering can build.

Stakeholder Mapping — Who Are Your "Customers"?

"Customer" isn't one person. In B2B especially, you're balancing multiple stakeholders, each with different goals:

StakeholderWhat they care aboutHow they evaluate
End User "Does this make my daily work easier?" Daily friction, time saved, satisfaction
Buyer / Economic Owner "Does this hit my business outcomes? Is the ROI clear?" Cost vs measurable impact (revenue, efficiency)
Executive Sponsor "Does this align with our strategic priorities?" Visibility, board-friendly metrics
IT / Procurement "Is this secure, compliant, integrable?" SOC2, SSO, data residency, integration paths
Legal / Compliance "Does this meet GDPR / HIPAA / industry rules?" Data handling, audit trail, contract terms
Champion / Internal Advocate "Will this make me look good?" Career impact, reputational risk
Detractor "Will this disrupt my territory or budget?" Threat to existing tools / processes / power
Critical insight: the buyer often isn't the user
In ChurnGuard: User = Customer Success Manager (uses the dashboard daily). Buyer = VP CS (signs the $100K contract). Executive sponsor = CRO (cares about revenue retention numbers). IT = needs Salesforce SSO. Legal = needs GDPR compliance.

All five must say YES for a deal to close. Your PRD, ICP, and GTM materials must address EACH separately.

Building a stakeholder map (1-page artifact)

For each stakeholder type:
  - Title / role
  - What they're measured on
  - What they want from us
  - What objections they'll raise
  - What proof / artifacts will satisfy them

The Continuous Discovery Cycle

Modern PM is NOT "do research once, then build." It's a continuous weekly habit:

Every week:
  - 5-10 customer/prospect conversations (30-45 min each)
  - 1-2 hours reviewing support tickets / Slack channels
  - 1 hour analyzing usage data
  - Write up patterns observed

Every month:
  - Synthesize: what's recurring? what's surprising?
  - Update PRD assumptions based on what you learned
  - Re-prioritize roadmap

Every quarter:
  - Larger retrospective: are we solving the right problems?
  - Refresh ICP if needed
  - Pricing experiments based on what customers value

Teresa Torres calls this "continuous discovery habits" — see her book/work for more depth.

⚠️ The "loudest customer" trap
Don't build for the customer who emails the most. Build for the PATTERN across many customers. One angry customer ≠ a product priority. Aggregate before deciding.

Customer Interview Techniques

1:1 customer interviews are the most powerful discovery tool. 30-45 min, recorded (with permission), notes synthesized within 24h.

Good interview questions

  • "Tell me about the last time you [had this problem]..." — concrete past behavior
  • "Walk me through your current process step by step." — reveals workarounds
  • "What's the worst part of your day related to X?" — finds pain
  • "How do you currently work around this?" — reveals existing solutions
  • "Have you ever tried to solve this? How did it go?" — past purchase intent
  • "How much time/money does this cost you per week?" — quantify pain
  • "Who else is affected by this?" — finds the buyer
  • "If you had a magic wand, what would change?" — surfaces wish lists carefully

Bad interview questions (avoid these)

  • "Would you use a product that does X?" — hypothetical = unreliable
  • "Do you like our product idea?" — politeness bias
  • "How much would you pay?" — people lie about pricing intent
  • "What features do you want?" — gets you a wish list, not the underlying need
  • "Would you pay $50/month for this?" — anchors them, biases response

Interview structure (45 min)

TimeSectionWhat to do
0-5 minRapport + context"Tell me about your role and what you do day-to-day."
5-15 minCurrent state / pain"Walk me through how you do X today. What's hard?"
15-25 minPast behavior"Tell me about the last time this went wrong. What happened?"
25-35 minWorkarounds + solutions tried"How have you tried to solve this? What worked / didn't?"
35-40 minMagnitude"How often does this happen? What does it cost you?"
40-45 minWrap + referrals"Who else should I talk to about this?"

"The Mom Test" — Avoiding False Validation

Famous PM book by Rob Fitzpatrick. Three rules:

  1. Talk about THEIR life, not your idea. Don't pitch.
  2. Ask about specific past behavior, not opinions about the future. "Tell me about the last time..." not "Would you...?"
  3. Talk less, listen more. Aim for 80% them, 20% you.

Why "Mom Test"? Even your mom won't tell you the truth about your idea — she's being supportive. Need techniques to avoid hearing what you want to hear.

The 3 sub-techniques

  • Get specific: "How often did you do that this week?" not "How often do you usually...?"
  • Probe for pain magnitude: "What did that cost you in time/money/stress?"
  • Surface commitment: "Have you ever paid for a tool to solve this?" — past spending = real pain

The 5 Whys Technique

When a customer says "I want X," ask "why" five times. Each layer peels back to the root need.

Worked example

Customer: "I want a button to export data to CSV."

Why? — "Because I need to share data with my finance team."

Why? — "Because they need to reconcile our numbers monthly."

Why? — "Because our Salesforce data and their bookings system don't match."

Why? — "Because there's no integration between the systems."

Why? — "Because each system was bought by a different team and IT never connected them."

REAL NEED: data reconciliation between disconnected systems
NOT what they asked for: "a CSV export button"

What you might actually build:
  - A scheduled report that auto-emails finance the reconciled data
  - OR an integration that prevents the divergence in the first place

The CSV button would technically satisfy the request — but miss the real problem.
Why this matters
Customers articulate SOLUTIONS (build me X), not problems. The PM's job is to translate solutions back into problems, then build the right solution. Often it's totally different from what was asked for.

A visualization of the customer's full experience around a workflow — including pain points and opportunities. Used to find where to insert your product.

Simple journey map (5 columns)

Stage What they're doing What they're feeling Pain points Opportunities

Investigate Pull together signals from CRM, support tickets Frustrated by tool-switching Manual + slow Auto-aggregated view

Aggregating Patterns Across Customers

One customer interview = an anecdote. Ten customers = a pattern. The discipline is in synthesizing.

The synthesis workflow

  1. After each interview: write 3-5 quotes + key observations within 24h (memory fades fast)
  2. Tag each observation by theme: "data integration pain", "CSV exports requested", "SOC2 question raised"
  3. Weekly: count tags. Themes that recur across 3+ customers = signal. Single mentions = noise.
  4. Monthly: rewrite key themes as problem statements with evidence count
  5. Quarterly: feed into roadmap

Example synthesis output

Theme: "Customer Success teams are reactive, not proactive"
  Mentioned by: 12 of 18 interviews (67%)
  Severity: HIGH (associated with $1M+ account losses)
  Existing workarounds: spreadsheets, manual CRM reports, weekly review meetings
  Magnitude: 8-15 hours/week per CSM spent on reactive triage

Theme: "Want CSV export from dashboard"
  Mentioned by: 3 of 18 interviews (17%)
  Real need (after 5 whys): data reconciliation with finance — not export per se
  → reframe as integration problem, not export problem

Tools: Dovetail, Notion, even a Google Sheet works. The discipline beats the tool.

Translating Customer Needs into PRDs (The Framework)

The core PM skill: turning raw customer input into a buildable spec. A 5-step framework:

Step 1 — Listen + Capture

Run interviews. Take quotes verbatim. Don't filter through your assumptions yet.

Step 2 — Aggregate + Theme

Find patterns across many conversations. What problems come up repeatedly? With what magnitude?

Step 3 — Reframe as Problem Statements

Convert "they want X" into "they have problem Y." Use the 5 Whys.

Wrong (solution): "Customers want a CSV export button"
Right (problem):  "Customer Success teams need to reconcile CRM data with finance
                   without manual copy-paste, currently costing 4 hours/week."

Step 4 — Hypothesize the Job-To-Be-Done

What is the customer "hiring" your product to do for them? Frame as outcome, not feature.

JTBD: "When my finance team needs reconciled customer data,
       I want to deliver it without manual work,
       so I can focus on customer relationships, not data plumbing."

Step 5 — Write the 1-Page PRD

Now you have everything to write a credible PRD:

PRD sectionComes from
Problem statementStep 3 reframed problem
HypothesisStep 4 JTBD
Target users / ICPPatterns from interviews — who said this most
Success metricWhat change would prove the problem is solved (quantify)
MVP scopeSmallest thing to test the hypothesis
Open questionsWhat you DON'T know yet — answer in next discovery round
The PRD is a hypothesis, not a contract
PMs new to lean methodology think the PRD has to be "right." It doesn't — it has to be CLEAR about what you're testing. After the spike, you update the PRD with real numbers. Hypothesis confirmed → expand. Falsified → pivot.

Step 5 — The 1-Page PRD

# ChurnGuard — 1-Page PRD

Problem
B2B SaaS CS teams lose $1M+ accounts unexpectedly. Existing tools don't
predict churn early enough; signals are fragmented across 5+ systems.
Result: 60% of CSM time is reactive triage of accounts already gone.

Hypothesis
We believe Series B+ B2B SaaS CSMs will adopt an early-warning agent
because they cannot afford another surprise million-dollar loss.
We'll know when 50% of pilot customers prevent at least one churn
in the first quarter.

Target Users
PRIMARY USER: Customer Success Manager (CSM) at $20M-$200M ARR SaaS
BUYER: VP Customer Success or CRO
EXECUTIVE SPONSOR: CFO/Board (revenue retention)

Success Metrics
- Precision: 60%+ on churn predictions (low false positives)
- Recall: 40%+ on actual churns (catch real problems)
- CSM time savings: 5+ hours/week reclaimed
- Customer save rate: 30% of flagged accounts prevented

Scope (MVP, 8 weeks)
IN: Watch 5 signals (login recency, ticket sentiment, NPS trend, payment delay, feature adoption)
IN: Weekly digest email with top 5 at-risk accounts + reasoning
IN: Salesforce integration (read-only)
OUT: Mobile app, automated outreach, predictive cause analysis (Phase 2)

Kill Criteria
- If precision < 40% after 4 weeks: pivot model approach
- If pilot customers don't see value at week 6: kill MVP
- If cost per prediction > $0.50: re-architect

Open Questions
- Do CSMs trust AI predictions, or want explainability? (test in alpha)
- Does intervention recommendation matter, or just early warning? (alpha)
- Salesforce vs HubSpot first? (50/50 by current ICP usage)

Notice: every line of the PRD traces back to specific evidence from interviews. Nothing is made up. That's a credible PRD.

Common Pitfalls & Anti-Patterns

Anti-patternWhat goes wrongFix
"Build what the customer asked for" Customer asks for CSV button; real need is integration. You ship the wrong thing. Always 5 Whys. Translate solutions to problems.
"Loudest customer wins" Roadmap shaped by 1-2 vocal customers, ignores patterns Aggregate across 10+ before prioritizing
"Sales-driven roadmap" Every deal needs "one more feature." Roadmap becomes spaghetti. Use a request log; prioritize by frequency + ICP fit, not deal size
"Talking only to existing customers" Misses why prospects DIDN'T buy. Survivorship bias. Interview lost-deal prospects too — incredibly informative
"Executive whim driving roadmap" "The CEO heard X at a dinner" → 2 sprints derailed Push back politely with data. "Let's validate with 3 customers first."
"Vibes-based decisions" "I think customers want this." No evidence trail. Every PRD must cite specific interview evidence + frequency
"Boil the ocean MVP" MVP balloons to 12 features. Ships in 9 months instead of 8 weeks. By then market changed. Cut scope ruthlessly. The MVP tests ONE hypothesis.
"Confirmation bias in interviews" You hear what you want to hear because you ask leading questions The Mom Test. Have a colleague review your interview transcripts.
"Skipping the spike for AI products" Write detailed PRD for AI feature you've never tested. Build for 3 months. Doesn't work. SPIKE FIRST. Always. AI behavior must be measured before specced.

AI-Specific Customer Dynamics

Customer expectations of AI products have unique distortions you must manage:

Customer expectationRealityHow PM handles it
"AI should be 100% accurate" It's never 100%. Hallucinations exist. Set explicit hallucination budget in PRD. Frame as "high accuracy + human safety net" — not "perfect."
"AI is magic, just throw a model at it" Real AI products need eval frameworks, RAG, prompt iteration, monitoring Educate during sales. Show eval results. Don't oversell.
"It worked great in the demo" Demos are cherry-picked. Production has long tail of edge cases. Closed alpha with REAL data. Set expectation: "v1 will surprise us."
"We don't trust AI for [high-stakes thing]" Reasonable concern. Build trust gradually. Start with human-in-loop. Expand autonomy as track record builds.
"How is this different from ChatGPT?" Vague positioning. They've used ChatGPT and don't see why pay. Sharp ICP, sharp problem statement. "This is for X doing Y, not general purpose."
"Privacy / data concerns" Real risk if not addressed. Compliance affects deal flow. Lead with data handling story. SOC2, HIPAA, BAA where applicable.
"What happens when the model gets deprecated?" Models DO get deprecated. Anthropic/OpenAI swap them every 6-12 months. Multi-vendor abstraction. Versioned prompts. Migration playbook in roadmap.
"Can you fine-tune on our data?" Often unnecessary; RAG + good prompting beats fine-tuning for most cases. Educate. Default to RAG. Fine-tune only when evals demand it.

The "AI honeymoon → disillusionment → real value" curve

Customer adoption typically goes:

Week 1-2: HONEYMOON ("This is amazing!")
              ↓
Week 3-6: DISILLUSIONMENT ("It got X wrong, can we trust it?")
              ↓
Week 7+:  REAL VALUE if trust is rebuilt with measurable wins
              OR
              CHURN if you don't manage the dip

PM job: design the disillusionment phase. Set expectations early.
       Plan trust-building wins for week 4-6. Show eval improvements.

Quantitative vs Qualitative Research

TypeAnswersTools
Qualitative (interviews)WHY users do things1:1 calls, observation, diary studies, Gong call recordings
Quantitative (analytics)WHAT users do, how oftenMixpanel, Amplitude, dashboards, SQL on event data

Use both. Quant tells you something happened; qual tells you why. The PM workflow:

1. QUANT spots an anomaly: "Activation rate dropped 20% last week"
2. QUAL investigates: 5 interviews with users who didn't activate
3. QUANT validates the hypothesis: "Was it the onboarding change? Yes — 40% drop in step 3"
4. Fix → measure → iterate

Kill Switches & Reversibility

Every AI product needs an "off button." Design considerations:

  • Per-tenant rate limits (cap tokens per day)
  • Daily budget alerts + auto-pause at threshold
  • Manual override / "stop all agents" command
  • Eval-driven auto-rollback (if quality drops > X%)
  • Confidence threshold gating (block low-confidence outputs)

  • SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) — for strategy

  • HEART (Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task success) — for metrics

Sample Questions + How to Answer

"How would you measure success for [AI feature]?"

Structure:
1. Clarify what the feature does (user / business goal)
2. Define quality metrics (precision/recall/groundedness)
3. Define adoption metrics (DAU, calls per user)
4. Define cost metrics (cost per call)
5. Define operational metrics (latency, uptime)
6. Pick a north star (1 metric that captures it all)
7. Mention kill criteria / counter-metrics

"Walk me through how you'd build [an AI product]."

Structure:
1. Hypothesis + ICP first (1-page brief)
2. Spike: 1-2 week engineering prototype
3. Measure: precision, cost, failure modes
4. Real PRD with concrete eval criteria
5. Build MVP
6. Closed alpha (5-10 design partners)
7. Iterate based on feedback
8. Public beta → GA
Mention: hallucination budget, cost-per-task, kill switches.

"How do you balance speed with quality on AI products?"

Structure:
1. Acknowledge the tradeoff (cost/quality/speed pick 2)
2. Set a quality floor with eval thresholds (won't ship below X)
3. Use kill switches for production safety
4. Iterate prompts before model changes (cheaper, faster)
5. Use closed alpha to find quality issues early
Reference: your real eval framework setup in P7.
